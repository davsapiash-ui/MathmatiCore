## 1. KEY TERMS & DEFINITIONS

**Concrete Manipulatives:** Physical, three-dimensional objects that can be held, manually rearranged, and used to teach specific mathematical concepts (Bassette et al., 2019, p. 391).

**Virtual Manipulatives:** Mathematics manipulatives represented in a digital format (app-based or web-based) on devices like tablets or computers, which students can touch, rotate, and drag to solve problems and build conceptual understanding (Long et al., 2023, p. 148).

**Alternative Manipulatives Definition:** Objects designed to represent explicitly and concretely mathematical ideas that are abstract (Bassette et al., 2020, p. 179).

**CRA (Concrete-Representational-Abstract):** A graduated instructional framework where students learn using concrete objects → pictorial drawings → abstract symbols (Long et al., 2023, p. 148).

**VRA (Virtual-Representational-Abstract):** A digital CRA adaptation where the concrete phase is replaced by on-screen virtual manipulatives (Long et al., 2023, p. 148).

**Self-Regulated Learning (SRL):** A cyclical, dynamic process of planning, monitoring, strategic adjustment, and reflection in which learners align their goals, cognitive strategies, and motivation (Agustin, 2026, p. 1).

**Co-Regulation:** The interactional shaping of regulatory conditions between a learner and a system, configuring explanations, prompts, and decision-making points without performing regulation on the learner's behalf (Agustin, 2026, p. 2).

**Productive Metacognitive Friction:** A regulatory dynamic that sustains learner-driven regulation by structuring how learners encounter challenge and discrepancy. Specifically, moments when discrepancy signals become interpretable cues that activate monitoring, control, and strategic choice (Agustin, 2026, pp. 1, 5).

**Learned Helplessness:** A psychological state where repeated difficulty and perceived lack of control over outcomes lead students to prematurely reduce effort, disengage from tasks, or engage in passive help-seeking behaviors (Miranda et al., 2025, p. 834).

**Number Sense:** An early numeracy skill and a broad, intuitive understanding of numbers that serves as a foundational base upon which more advanced mathematics skills are built (Dueker & Desai, 2022, p. 85).

**Feedback Types:**
- *Task Feedback:* Comments directly on how well assignments are understood or performed (Knoop-van Campen et al., 2023, p. 3452).
- *Process Feedback:* Comments on the strategies, processes, and cognitive mechanisms children use to handle assignments (Knoop-van Campen et al., 2023, p. 3448).
- *Personal Feedback:* Comments on the student as a person — considered less effective because it does not contain specific directions to improve learning (Knoop-van Campen et al., 2023, p. 3448).

## 2. UDL IN EARLY MATHEMATICS (Grades 1–3)

### Engagement
- Explicit Instruction format: modeling (think-alouds) → guided practice → independent practice (Dueker & Desai, 2022, p. 90).
- Fast instructional pace with active responses (nodding, verbalizing) maintains student attention (Dueker & Desai, 2022, p. 95).
- Manipulatives increase student engagement, motivation, and attention (Long et al., 2023, p. 148).
- Personalization features support engagement when they authentically integrate students' interests (Agustin, 2026, p. 11).

### Representation
- Multiple representations (solved examples, worksheets, virtual manipulatives) bring clarity to abstract computational concepts (Dueker & Desai, 2022, pp. 96–97).
- Graduated representational sequences provide a cognitive bridge between quantities and Arabic numerals (Bassette et al., 2020, p. 187).
- Visual decomposition (simplifying visual presentation) reduces confusion and helps students attend to relevant stimuli (Dueker & Desai, 2022, p. 90).
- **Video modeling** is recommended because it focuses attention on specific aspects of a skill and filters out distracting environmental stimuli (Dueker & Desai, 2022, p. 90).

### Action & Expression
- Multiple physical actions — touching, dragging, drawing — promote active participation (Dueker & Desai, 2022, p. 98).
- Touch screens provide an effective mode of action for learners lacking the dexterity to use a mouse (Dueker & Desai, 2022, p. 93).
- Students can demonstrate learning by writing calculated answers or holding work up to a camera for immediate diagnostic feedback (Dueker & Desai, 2022, p. 92).

## 3. ACCOMMODATIONS FOR DIVERSE LEARNERS

### Attention Needs
- Managing multiple physical pieces during concrete manipulative use can distract students and increase extraneous cognitive load (Bassette et al., 2020, p. 180).
- Video modeling is highly recommended for students with attention needs because it isolates relevant stimuli (Dueker & Desai, 2022, p. 90).
- A systematic **time delay of 10 seconds** before prompting allows students with slower processing speeds to attempt actions independently (Bouck et al., 2017, p. 322).

### ASD
- Students with ASD face challenges due to difficulties with reading, working memory, procedural calculations, and processing mathematical language (Bassette et al., 2019, p. 391).
- Modified Schema-Based Instruction (SBI) is effective for teaching word problem solving to students with ASD and moderate intellectual disability (Root et al., 2017, p. 43).
- SBI replaces textual mnemonics with a **visual self-instruction sheet** in task analysis format containing pictures alongside text for each step (Root et al., 2017, p. 44).
- Color-coded **graphic organizers (schema diagrams)** help students organize information and visualize comparisons (Root et al., 2017, p. 44).
- Systematic, least-to-most prompting (verbal → specific verbal → model) guides students with ASD through procedural steps (Root et al., 2017, p. 47).

### Virtual vs. Concrete Manipulatives
- Students with ASD complete more steps independently and efficiently per minute using app-based vs. concrete manipulatives (Bassette et al., 2019, p. 391).
- During concrete sessions, students with ASD often exhibit interfering behaviors (fidgeting, stacking), increasing task completion time (Bassette et al., 2019, p. 403).
- App-based manipulatives display Arabic numerals alongside blocks, helping students connect quantities to symbols (Bassette et al., 2020, p. 187).
- Virtual interfaces reduce response effort by automating lower-level procedural mechanics, such as **automatically breaking up a tens rod into 10 ones when dragged to another column** (Bassette et al., 2020, p. 187).
- Virtual manipulatives are less conspicuous than concrete manipulatives, reducing social stigmatization (Bouck et al., 2017, p. 317).

## 4. ASSESSMENT & DIAGNOSTIC APPROACHES

- Supervised ML classifiers can effectively classify learned helplessness using behavioral interaction logs and academic profiles (Miranda et al., 2025, p. 834).
- **EBM (Explainable Boosting Machines):** High performance and interpretability — key predictors: GWA, math anxiety, time spent, problem-solving success (Miranda et al., 2025, p. 834).
- The interaction between emotional variables (anxiety) and cognitive variables (problem-solving success) is highly influential in predicting learned helplessness (Miranda et al., 2025, p. 840).
- **Real-time clustering** (K-means + Gap Statistics) can divide students into homogeneous profiles based on diagnostic item responses (Nazaretsky et al., 2022, p. 11).
- **Q-Matrix mapping** enables systems to automatically build pedagogically meaningful explanations of each cluster's strengths and weaknesses (Nazaretsky et al., 2022, p. 1).
- Providing teachers with competency-based cluster explanations has a statistically significant positive effect on planning personalized follow-up sequences (Nazaretsky et al., 2022, p. 7).
- Formative assessment classifies student errors into: fact errors / component-skill errors / strategy errors — guiding specific re-teaching (Dueker & Desai, 2022, p. 86).
- Standardized diagnostics (e.g., KeyMath-3) identify grade-equivalent performance ceilings in multi-digit calculation (Bouck et al., 2017, p. 318).
- Real-time teacher dashboards make variations in student progress and task completion times visible, providing objective performance overviews (Knoop-van Campen et al., 2023, p. 3457).
- Dashboard data helps improve teacher judgments over subjective perceptions, leading to more equal distribution of process and task feedback (Knoop-van Campen et al., 2023, pp. 3450–3457).

## 5. PRACTICES THE RESEARCH EXPLICITLY WARNS AGAINST

- ❌ **Overspecifying Pathways:** Systems must not over-specify pathways or automate strategy selections, as this diminishes the decision points through which SRL develops and leads to passive compliance (Agustin, 2026, p. 8).
- ❌ **Over-Automation:** Resolving task discrepancies before learners notice them creates a false "illusion of mastery" and displaces monitoring and control (Agustin, 2026, p. 7).
- ❌ **Short-Term Performance Optimization:** Optimization driven purely by short-term performance or novelty collapses critical decision-making points and bypasses essential practice (Agustin, 2026, p. 11).
- ❌ **Rote Responding Without Context:** Instruction without connecting concepts to the student's environment leads to memorized, rote responding rather than conceptual understanding (Dueker & Desai, 2022, p. 85).
- ❌ **Passive, Non-Interactive Viewing:** Requiring students to watch demonstrations for too long without active participation risks them tuning out (Dueker & Desai, 2022, p. 94).
- ❌ **Exclusive Reliance on Personal Feedback:** Only providing personal praise (e.g., "You are doing great!") is less effective and does not help students reach their learning goals (Knoop-van Campen et al., 2023, p. 3448).
- ❌ **Subjective, Unmonitored Grouping:** Dividing students into permanent groups based on intuition or summative grades alone ignores dynamic cognitive performance and fails to address specific transient cognitive gaps (Nazaretsky et al., 2022, pp. 3–4).
